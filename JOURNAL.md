# 3/31/2026 4 PM - Rework design based on review feedback

_Time spent: 4.5h_

This update mostly happened because of the feedback I got on the last review. The main issue was that the project wasn't complex for Tier 2, and was too expensive for Tier 1. Additionally, the reviewer pointed out some key cost-cutting measures, which I did feel were really doable. So I had to rethink the direction a bit instead of just tweaking small things.

Earlier, it was mostly just a display and one sensor. Based on the suggestion, I added two more sensors;  the STK8328 accelerometer, and the AGR10 pressure sensor. It also made me deal with two types of interfaces, namely I2C and analog, which added to the complexity in a meaningful way.

![image](https://stasis.hackclub-assets.com/images/1774975737277-wv11vb.png)

Another suggestion was about my PCBA choice and improving how parts were sourced. I made the OLED setup simpler by using a module instead of bare SMD ribbon cable, which got me rid of the complex charge pump system and related.  The feedback about the STM32 and PCBA cost also made me rethink the microcontroller choice. I switched to the XIAO RP2040, sourced through Robu and will be later soldered by me. Not getting the OLED and MCU through PCBA will reduce the cost, since PCBA will buy and solder two of each component, something that is not cost-effective at all.

![image](https://stasis.hackclub-assets.com/images/1774975758979-h6n8nm.png)

I also updated the firmware to work with all the new components, and it now used CircuitPython. The pressure part is a bit rough since it's based on a simple and uncalibrated conversion for now.

![image](https://stasis.hackclub-assets.com/images/1774975788650-6aggx7.png)

Overall, I think I addressed the feedback properly. The project is now clearly more complex than before, while also being more practical and cheaper to build, relative to the components used.

# 3/25/2026 11 AM - PCB Routing and firmware

_Time spent: 3.25h_

PCB routing was actually pretty easy. There were only 3 main components, the MCU (STM32), the OLED display, and the AHT20. The rest are pull up resistors, and a whole array of decoupling capacitors. Since this is not my very first time using them, I am aware that they should be placed as close as possible to the pins. Fortunately, I am getting this as a PCBA, so size is not a problem. I went with 0605 size, so I could fit them all fairly easily around the pins. A dual ground plane helped for routing, noise, as well as aesthetics.  Regarding the firmware, I just wanted a proof of concept, since this is my first time using an STM32, and therfore I know next to nothing about it. Using my basic knowledge of MicroPython, and some help from an AI, I created the most basic firmware. This is subject to most amount of change, for afformentioned reasons. Adding some ~~propoganda~~ branding of my own, and the PCB routing along with the full design is finally done!

# 3/25/2026 8 AM - Schematic

_Time spent: 2.5h_

After less time than I expected, but more components than I expected, I have finished the schematic! I thought it would just be connecting components together, but there were a lot of small details.

The STM32 module itself was straightforward since most pins were already labelled clearly; although my first time needing pull up resistors, cause I previously only worked with Picos (they have this in-built). The AHT20 sensor was also relatively simple.

The OLED display was the behemoth of confusion. It had a lot extra pins that I didn't understand, especially the "charge pump" ones: C1P, C1N, C2P, and C2N. I had to refer to the datasheet multiple times (and other sources to look up sample wiring) and still wasn't fully confident about whether I understood everything correctly. Adding the numerous required capacitors and the resistor made the schematic look even more complicated.

The decoupling capacitors were also frustrating to draw up. Every component seemed to need more than one! I ended up following general guidelines, like adding a mix of small and big ones.

I had to go through this big mess multiple times, checking connections and making sure nothing was left; eventually everything seemed to be connected properly.

# 3/24/2026 12 PM - Researching parts and finalizing BOM

_Time spent: 3h_

At the start of this project, I wanted to use the bare RP2040 SMD version. It is used in a lot of projects and I was fairly familiar with RPI Picos. Once I actually started researching how to use the bare chip though, I realised that it needed a lot of extra components like flash memory, crystal oscillator, power stuff, and a bunch of capacitors. This was beyond the Hermes' simple I2C board.

Therefore, I switched to the STM32 module (STM32F103C8T6, specifically, since that is available in JLC PCB fab). For the sensor, I chose the AHT20 because it was popular and cheap. The OLED display choice was a lot more confusing. I wanted this to be a fully assembled PCB, since that is cheaper than ordering components from local sellers. So I deeply searched through JLC's parts library, keeping in mind economic PCBA and trying my best to avoid exteneded components to save on cost. That particulary was a very big problem for the OLED, since it had too many variables like having I2C, being eligible for economic PCBA, and not having assembly difficulty of "high" (this was a new one for me, but very important cause each such component adds more than 3 USD to it's assembly). I also had to add a whole array of decoupling caps and resistors for making these work, something I will ellaborate in the next journal.
